Supervised Student Theses

My students are my team. I rely on them heavily for developing new ideas and for bringing new perspectives and inspiration into my work. For that reason, good supervision is very important to me.

I also want to encourage a team spirit among my students. In addition to student activities organized at the institute, such as the summer BBQ, Christmas party, and Christmas market event, I have lunch with my students every two weeks. This gives everyone time to connect, get to know each other, and talk about topics beyond day-to-day work.

Stochastic Model Predictive Control for Consumer-Driven District Heating Networks

Student: Lara Koppel
Status: Finished
Supervisors: Ben Spoek, M.Sc.; Dr. Sebastian Stemmler
Auditor: Prof. Dr.-Ing. Dirk Müller
Description

The heating sector accounts for a large part of global energy consumption and remains heavily dependent on fossil fuels. To achieve climate neutrality, a transition toward modern district heating networks with reduced temperatures is required, as these networks enable the integration of distributed renewable energy and urban waste heat. Efficiently managing these systems is complex due to the dynamic interactions between distributed consumers and centralized producers. This complexity requires advanced control strategies such as model predictive control. Deploying these predictive architectures is challenging, as prolonged hydraulic transport delays lead to the accumulation of temporally correlated weather forecast errors.

This work proposes a demand-side stochastic model predictive control framework and evaluates it against a deterministic baseline. The study focuses on decentralized building control by abstracting the network interface into a constant transport delay, placing supply-side dispatch and detailed hydraulic network dynamics outside the scope. To handle uncertainty, the disturbance model integrates Ensemble Model Output Statistics with a first-order autoregressive error model. This generates calibrated probabilistic forecasts that capture the autocorrelation of prediction errors. An augmented state-space formulation propagates the forecast error variance via closed-form linear covariance equations. This enables probabilistic constraint adaptation, dynamically replacing static safety margins.

Closed-loop control simulations across varying network transport delays, building types, and seasonal conditions indicate that the operational advantage over the certainty-equivalence deterministic baseline is governed by the variance of the state covariance matrix. This advantage is amplified by the extension of the hydraulic transport horizon, meteorological input volatility, and the speed of the building’s transient thermal response. Under amplifying conditions, relative energy savings of up to 43.7% are achieved compared to the deterministic benchmark at equivalent comfort constraint satisfaction. The results suggest that uncertainty-aware demand-side control is particularly beneficial for thermally sensitive buildings with limited passive thermal buffering.

Consumer-Driven Decentralized Model Predictive Control for District Heating Applications

Student: Jaspar Paulus
Status: Finished
Supervisors: Ben Spoek, M.Sc.
Auditor: Prof. Dr.-Ing. Dirk Müller
Description

Decarbonizing the building heat sector is a prerequisite for achieving the greenhouse gas reduction targets of the Paris Climate Agreement. District heating networks offer a promising pathway, enabling the large-scale integration of renewable and waste heat sources while leveraging economies of scale in heat generation. Realizing this potential requires control strategies that go beyond conventional rule-based control, which cannot fully exploit the thermal flexibility of the network and the connected buildings.

Model predictive control (MPC) offers a principled framework for demand-oriented, efficiency-optimized district heating operation. Centralized approaches that jointly optimize the entire system are, however, computationally intractable for real-world systems due to the high dimensionality and nonlinear dynamics of the coupled network-consumer system. This thesis proposes and investigates a novel consumer-driven, decentralized two-level MPC architecture. Each consumer employs an individual MPC to schedule its own heat demand predictively while adhering to comfort constraints and cost considerations. The consolidated demand forecasts are coordinated by a single producer MPC, which determines the optimal thermal energy input trajectory at the energy hub to satisfy all consumer demands simultaneously, accounting for transport delays, thermal losses, and the thermal inertia of the distribution network.

To evaluate this concept, a complete simulation environment is developed from scratch in Python. It encompasses a white-box Plug Flow Pipe network model capturing transport delay and thermal losses; a data-driven autoregressive model with exogenous inputs pipe model serving as the producer MPC prediction model; a white-box thermal building model coupled with a consumer MPC; a producer MPC that translates the sum of consumer heat orders into an optimized supply temperature trajectory; and a classical proportional-integral controlled reference system for comparison. The proposed system is evaluated on a simplified two-district network, each district comprising four thermal zones, which collectively equate to approximately thirty single-family houses, over two representative one-week simulation periods in winter and spring, each investigated under dual and single night-setback configurations.

Within the scope of this study, the results demonstrate various advantages of the consumer-driven MPC over the proportional-integral reference. Accumulated thermal comfort violations are reduced by approximately 73% in spring and 81% in winter. Network heat losses decrease by 5% to 8% in spring and by 16% to 17% in winter, reducing the total energy input at the energy hub by 1.3% to 2.3% in spring and 2.7% to 2.9% in winter, respectively.

Off-Design Operation of Heat Pumps in Energy Hubs: Analysis of the Interactions Between Heat Pump Design and System Design

Student: Peter Nölke
Status: Finished
Supervisors: Sebastian Ostlender, M.Sc.; Ben Spoek, M.Sc.
Auditor: Prof. Dr.-Ing. Dirk Müller
Description

In order to reduce greenhouse gas emissions in central heating supply, fossil fuels must be replaced by renewable energy sources. One way of doing this is to integrate electrically powered heat pumps into energy hubs. The operating behavior of heat pumps is heavily dependent on the operating point, which is referred to as off-design behavior. In off-design operation, both the efficiency and the available condenser power vary. The effects of this off-design behavior on the operation of energy hubs have not yet been comprehensively analyzed.

In this thesis, a dynamic simulation model of an energy hub with 1.3 MW total thermal capacity is developed based on validated sub-models. This enables a holistic view of the effects of off-design behavior on the energy hub. The model includes a heat pump as the primary energy generator and a combined heat and power plant as the secondary energy generator. The off-design behavior of the heat pump is mapped using a detailed map model that describes the condenser power and the efficiency of the heat pump as a function of the operating point and design parameters. The design parameters of the heat pump are the design point, the refrigerant, and the flowsheet. For comparison, a simplified model is used that maps the operating behavior using the Carnot efficiency and does not take any design parameters into account. Both approaches are analyzed in annual simulations with varying design capacities of the heat pump and combined heat and power unit. The simulation results are evaluated using the seasonal performance factor of the heat pump, the overall efficiency of the energy hub, and the proportion of renewable thermal energy.

The results show that both the seasonal performance factor and the overall efficiency depend on the power output of the heat pump. The magnitude of this influence depends on the combination of design parameters. Depending on the design capacity of the heat pump, combinations with different emphases should be selected. For small heat pump design capacity, the focus is on a large peak power of the condenser in order to achieve a high share of renewable thermal energy. For large heat pump design capacity, the seasonal performance factor becomes more important, as it contributes significantly to optimizing the overall efficiency of the energy hub.

This work demonstrates that knowledge of the detailed off-design behavior of the heat pump is relevant for system design. In order to realize potential energy savings, the design parameters of the heat pump must be taken into account in the design.
